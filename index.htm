Research Experience

  • Improvement of Small-Signal Stability Margins of AC Grids Using Controlled Power Injection Devices: Controlled power injection devices such as HVDC, FACTS devices can modulate their active and reactive power injections fast and independently. The objective is to study the effect of this capability of these devices on the small-signal stability margins of an AC grid. It can be shown that with simple and intuitive control strategies, these devices can improve the small-signal stability margins irrespective of the size, topology and the operating conditions. The specific applicability of these control strategies are investigated and they are found to be particularly beneficial for specific kind of disturbances.
  • Coherence Based Real Time Filtering of Distribution Level Frequency Measurements: Recent advancements in digital computation and power-electronic devices have improved the applicability of controlled power injection devices for distributed control of power systems. Although these devices can improve the system stability margins with specific control strategies, the measurement of feedback signal becomes a challenge in low voltage due to poor signal-to-noise ratio. The applicability of "learning-based" coherence filtering for filtering of distribution level frequency measurements have been studied. It has been seen that the filtering strategy can be implemented in real-time with inconsiderable filtering delay and phase distortions.
  • Robust Grid Connectivity Stability Criterion of Controlled Power Injection Devices: The advent of controlled power injection devices happened with the objective of improved control and performance of AC grids. While these devices are usually designed to improve the stability margins of the system, the presence of non-linearities often results in catastrophic failures at some specific operating points. The objective is to provide a simple, robust, easy-to-check grid connectivity criterion for these devices. The proposed conditions can be easily checked using time-domain simulations and frequency scanning techniques.
